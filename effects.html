Effects of Air Pollution

Air pollution affects both human health and the environment. Its effects can be classified into short-term and long-term impacts.

Short-term Effects:

  • Respiratory Infections: Exposure to polluted air increases the risk of respiratory infections such as bronchitis and pneumonia.
  • Eye and Skin Irritations: Pollutants like ozone can cause burning eyes and skin irritations.
  • Reduced Lung Function: High levels of air pollution reduce lung capacity, making breathing more difficult, especially for vulnerable populations like children and the elderly.

Long-term Effects:

  • Chronic Respiratory Diseases: Prolonged exposure to pollutants like particulate matter (PM2.5) increases the risk of developing chronic bronchitis, asthma, and other lung diseases.
  • Cardiovascular Diseases: Air pollution is linked to increased rates of heart attacks, strokes, and other cardiovascular conditions.
  • Premature Deaths: The WHO estimates that air pollution causes around 7 million premature deaths globally each year, particularly from heart disease, stroke, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and lung cancer.

Environmental Effects:

  • Global Warming: Pollutants such as CO2 and methane (CH4) contribute to the greenhouse effect, leading to global warming and climate change.
  • Ozone Depletion: Chemicals like chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) damage the ozone layer, increasing the risk of skin cancer and other health issues due to higher ultraviolet (UV) radiation exposure.
  • Acid Rain: Sulfur dioxide (SO2) and nitrogen oxides (NOx) can lead to acid rain, which harms ecosystems, contaminates water bodies, and damages crops and infrastructure.

The cumulative effects of air pollution are devastating for both human health and the planet. It is crucial to take urgent action to reduce emissions and promote cleaner, more sustainable living practices.
